Dr. Norman Borlaug

The man who saved a billion lives

Dr. Norman Borlaug, third from the left, trains biologists in Mexico on how to increase wheat yields - part of his life-long war on hunger.

Here's a timeline of Dr. Borlaug's life:

  • 1914 - Born in Cresco, Iowa, to a farming family, where he developed an early appreciation for agriculture.
  • 1933 - Left his familyâ€™s farm to attend the University of Minnesota. He studied forestry, supported by a Depression-era program known as the National Youth Administration.
  • 1935 - Had to pause his studies due to financial constraints. Joined the Civilian Conservation Corps, where he helped improve living conditions for starving Americans.
  • 1937 - Completed his degree and began working for the U.S. Forestry Service, gaining experience in scientific research.
  • 1938 - Married Margret Gibson. Inspired by mentor Elvin Charles Stakman, he returned to university to study plant pathology and genetics.
  • 1944 - Moved to Mexico to head a new agricultural program, developing high-yield, disease-resistant wheat varieties. His work was pivotal in fighting global hunger.
  • 1953 - Successfully bred a dwarf wheat variety with higher yields, revolutionizing agriculture in Mexico and later in other countries.
  • 1970 - Awarded the Nobel Peace Prize for his contributions to alleviating world hunger through agricultural innovation.
  • 1983 - Worked in Africa, helping seven countries dramatically improve their maize and sorghum production.
  • 2005 - Advocated for the use of genetically modified crops to meet the food demands of a growing population, emphasizing the safety and necessity of the technology.
  • 2009 - Passed away at the age of 95, leaving behind a legacy of saving over a billion lives through his groundbreaking agricultural work.
